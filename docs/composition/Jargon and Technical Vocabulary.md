# Jargon and Technical Vocabulary


!!!tldr "Rule"
        Be intentional about the technical words you introduce. Avoid jargon that forms a barrier to understanding.


!!!tldr "Rule"
    Make consistent reference to technical concepts.

Learners should emerge from a course on Brilliant able to speak knowledgeably on a subject. However, concepts are more important than technical words.

The purpose of attaching technical name to important ideas and processes is to ease referencing them. Once you establish a technical name, use a glossary card to reinforce it, and use the term consistently.

Sometimes, blocks of text can be shortened considerably by making a definition, even if the definition is not a global concept within the course. Definitions take up RAM in the learner's brain, but if making a definition streamlines the text or the statement of a question considerably, it's worth it.

!!! failure "Example"
    The probability of drawing exactly one red ball on two attempts is the probability of picking a red ball on the first attempt and picking a blue ball on the second, plus the probability of picking a blue ball on the first attempt and a red ball on the second.

!!! success "Example"
    The probability of drawing exactly one red ball on two attempts is $P(\text{pick 1 is red})P(\text{pick 2 is blue})$, plus $P(\text{pick 1 is blue})P(\text{pick 2 is red})$.

Technical names should be reserved for only the most important concepts, not details that arise rarely. Someone can memorize the names and characteristics of 500 birds without understanding how they fly or how their metabolism works. Jargon-y text is the result of attaching names to ideas that are not central.

Jargon can make a simple and important idea impenetrable. Do not introduce jargon in a Brilliant lesson.
