# Modularity

<aside>
📏 Ideas in a lesson are presented in a linear stream of thought. A user should be able to glance back through a block and easily identify its key points and arguments.

</aside>

In a textbook, paragraphs advance ideas. In a Brilliant lesson, we develop ideas in much smaller chunks. A Brilliant lesson is a conversation in which the learner participates by answering questions, manipulating interactives, and solving problems.

The key to good conversation is balance among the participants. Saying too much or too little on your turn is a conversation killer. The aim is to say just enough to move the conversation forward.

To begin writing the lesson, it may help to envision an actual conversation with someone who is learning the subject. What questions would they ask? How would you know they are understanding?

Once you chart out your main points, group sentences according to which thoughts are best digested together. Deliver one idea at a time. Give the learner frequent opportunities to demonstrate that they follow.

Keep in mind the block structure of lessons makes it difficult for learners to reference variables in previous blocks. If you are carrying over ideas from a previous block, it is helpful to have something to reference in the current block (like context you provide at the beginning of the block).
