# Lesson-writing Guidance and Conventions

>The purpose of this guide is to lay out format and style conventions for writing on Brilliant. The lessons we design are the bones of what we do, but consistent and clear language and format contribute to their clarity and sparkle.

The draw of Brilliant is our ideas. Our medium, whether writing or images or interactives, can help deliver these ideas, or get in the way. Our best writing is direct and easy to read.

The principles listed below reflect how formatting and language should support the ideas in our lessons. The more specific guidance in the four thematic sections are considered to be logical conclusions from these principles.

!!! tldr
    This is a non-exhaustive reference. It is a living document that is updated regularly. If the guidance below doesn't address your specific problem, asking in the Slack channel `#editorial-lounge` is also encouraged.**

## Composition Principles

**Reading flow** — Layout of questions, text, images and interactives supports and complements a smooth reading experience.

**Cadence and timing** — Each step is a single, complete thought. Each block is a complete achievement.

**Questions roles** — Lightweight questions are prospective, setting up lesson concepts and intuition. MCQs ask learners to apply ideas introduced explicitly in a lesson.

**Information field** — The learner's decisions should be as simple as possible. They should never be guessing about what to do.

**Minimize disruption** — Screen clears should happen at expected junctures. Glossary cards and asides are meant to reduce friction, not momentum.

## Formatting Principles

**Sentences** flow from beginning to end. We never write sentences that double back, or qualify statements while in the process of making them.

**Punctuation** sets the cadence of our writing and delineates ambiguous situations. We should do this as visually as we can.

Text **formatting** and **layout** draw attention to what's important. Emphasis everywhere is emphasis nowhere.

Our **language** is accurate and precise, but never pedantic.

**Numbers, equations**, and **pseudocode** are main players. We want them to have a tactile quality, like they're natural objects for the learner to manipulate.

### Sentences and Punctuation

The purpose of a **sentence** is to advance a complete thought. There's no magic point where a sentence needs to end, but the best sentences are direct and purposeful.

**Punctuation** is a tool to clarify the meaning of a sentence with pauses and separation. It can also be used to suggest a rhythm, but this is strictly less important than clarity. .

### Text Formatting and Layout

The purpose of formatting is to promote reading comprehension.

We make intentional use of whitespace and group sentences that form a step. The learner shouldn't have to perform a close reading to figure out the structure of our arguments in their head, our layout should show it.

Key points that would otherwise get buried in a wall of text can be surfaced with boldface type, bulleted lists, line breaks, and the like.

Use emphasis sparingly — emphasis everywhere is emphasis nowhere.

### Language

At Brilliant, our business is teaching people how to think about complex things. The narrator is the learner's trusted guide in their quest for understanding, and they rely on us for the clarity and quality of our explanations.

The learner is an active participant in the lesson and we write in the spirit of collaboration.

The words we choose should make it as easy as possible to understand our ideas. Learners shouldn't have to spend time to pick apart our meaning — they need that time for the concepts.

Our language is vivid and precise, leaving no question about the claims we make or when they apply.

### Formatting Numbers, Math, and Code

On Brilliant, numbers, equations, and pseudocode are main players. We want them to have a tactile feel, like they're natural objects for the learner to play with and manipulate. To promote this, numbers, equations, and pseudocode are always formatted — without exception. The format signals to the learner "here are the things you're working with".

Numbers, equations, and pseudocode are the language we use to convey precise meaning. They can appear inline with the written word or set apart, if they require closer inspection. Our symbolic conventions promote legibility and understanding.

Numbers, equations, and pseudocode should not be disruptive in form or appearance. This means we want to be as consistent as possible across all courses.

If you need to refer to a sophisticated expression many times, you should establish a name or symbol for it using a centered environment and use that for repeated inline reference.
