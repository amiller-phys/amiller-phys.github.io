
In some Brilliant lessons, we attach technical importance to strings of letters that are not necessarily words. Working with these objects is central to the learning experience, so they are formatted in the math environment.

For example, in the computational biology course, nucleotide sequences are given to and manipulated by the learner to solve problems.

!!! check "Example"

    The "TATA box" is a canonical example — a short nucleotide sequence $\tt{TATAAAA}$ found on the coding strand just before the start of the gene.

In a logic course, the objects a learner manipulates could be logic gates:

!!! check "Example"

    The simplest half-adder design incorporates \(\mathbf{XOR}\) and $\mathbf{AND}.$

Similar to the guidance for equations, strings are punctuated as if they were nouns in a sentence, and the punctuation is typeset in the math environment to avoid hanging punctuation.

In a lesson you're writing, if the learner will manipulate objects represented by strings, math formatting is probably correct, but because these cases are rare, we encourage you to post about your use case in the Slack channel `#editorial-lounge`.
