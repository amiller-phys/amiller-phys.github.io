---
title: Operations and Special Functions
---

!!!tldr "Rule"
    Learners can digest lessons across the Brilliant library without encountering friction due to a different notations.


A lot of mathematical notation is standard (`+` will always stand for addition). It would a waste of time to make an exhaustive list of operations here. What follows is a list of operations where there's some choice in how their represented, and where Brilliant lessons fall

- A products of two numbers, or a scalar product of two vectors, is indicated by `\cdot`

!!! success "Example"
    $3 \cdot 4 = 12$

!!! failure "Example"
    $3 \times 4 = 12$

- A product between a number and a variable needs no explicit operand. Multiplication is understood.
- Division should generally be represented by `/`, but if you are writing for a novice audience, the `÷` symbol is okay. Be aware that `÷` can be confused for a `+` on the site.
- The cross product between vectors is represented by `\times`
- Use `\log` for the natural log. If other than natural log, write the base as a subscript: $\log_2$
- Use `e^{}` for the exponential function, unless the expression in the exponent is exceptionally complicated, in which case use `\exp()` with a glossary card that describes the notation.
- $\csc, \sec, \cot$ and $\tan$: These functions are not as familiar or as fundamental as $\sin$ and $\cos$. When you use them, you are relying on a learner's ability to recall their definitions — not their reasoning or intuition. When you do use them, acknowledge that some people are not going to remember, for example, if $\sin$ or $\cos$ is in the numerator of $\tan.$ Using glossary cards to provide definitions of these functions is a good solution.
- Inverse trig functions should be formatted like so: $\cos^{-1}$
- For other special functions, use standard symbols — $\text{erf}, \Gamma, J_\alpha,$ and others.

The symbols we use for certain operations may be slightly different for different audiences. For example, a course on partial differential equations can assume more familiarity with different notations than a course on algebra. If a course you're writing needs to deviate from the standard symbology above, raising it on Slack in `#editorial-lounge` is a good idea.
